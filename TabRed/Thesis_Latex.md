\section{Introduction\label{sec:Intro}}
Machine learning is increasingly used to support decision making in areas such as credit scoring, insurance, and fraud detection. Over the last years, there has been a clear shift from relatively simple and transparent models toward more complex approaches such as ensemble methods and deep neural networks. These models often achieve superior predictive performance, but this comes at a cost: their internal decision process is typically difficult to interpret. As a result, they are often described as \emph{black-box} models. \cite{barredo_arrieta_explainable_2020, lundberg_unified_2017, murdoch_definitions_2019}

In many practical settings, predictive performance alone is not sufficient. A prediction is often only useful if people can understand it well enough to act on it with confidence. This is especially relevant in high-stakes contexts, where model outputs may affect individuals, organizations, or important operational decisions. In such settings, practitioners, domain experts, and regulators increasingly expect not only accurate predictions, but also insight into how those predictions are produced. This growing demand has led to the rise of \emph{Explainable AI} (XAI), a broad collection of methods that aim to make complex machine learning models more understandable. XAI techniques provide information about which features influenced a prediction and how they contributed to the model's output, thereby helping humans to interpret and assess model behavior. \cite{barredo_arrieta_explainable_2020, ribeiro_why_2016, rudin_stop_2019}

Among the many XAI methods proposed in recent years, two of the most widely used are LIME and SHAP. LIME (\emph{Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations}) explains a prediction by approximating the black-box model locally with a simpler interpretable model, typically using perturbed samples around the instance of interest. \cite{ribeiro_why_2016} SHAP (\emph{Shapley Additive Explanations}) assigns contribution values to input features based on ideas from cooperative game theory. \cite{lundberg_unified_2017} Both methods are popular because they can be applied to complex predictive models and provide feature-based explanations that are relatively intuitive to interpret. At the same time, both methods introduce an additional layer between the model and the human user: rather than inspecting the model directly, one inspects an explanation generated by another procedure. This makes it important to critically assess not only whether explanations are informative, but also whether they are reliable.

There are several ways to judge the quality of an explanation. Common dimensions include faithfulness (does the explanation reflect the model's true behavior), interpretability (is it understandable), computational cost, and \emph{robustness or stability}. Stability is the focus of this thesis.

Intuitively, stability means that explanations should not change drastically without a good reason. \cite{alvarez-melis_robustness_2018} If two very similar inputs lead to very different explanations, users may lose trust, and it becomes difficult to rely on explanations in practice. For simple models, stability is easier to reason about. Consider a linear model
\[
\hat{y} = \beta_0 + \sum_{j=1}^{p} \beta_j x_j.
\]
Here, the coefficients $\beta_j$ directly represent how features influence the prediction. If the model is retrained and the coefficients change, the change in reasoning is explicit. For complex models, we need an extra layer: an XAI method translates the model into an explanation. This extra layer introduces new uncertainty. They may reflect a genuine change in the model's behavior, but they may also arise from approximation error, sensitivity to perturbations, or other forms of methodological variability. Stability is therefore crucial for the practical trustworthiness of XAI.

Much of the discussion around stability starts from a \emph{static} setting: the model and data are assumed fixed, and stability is tested by running the explainer multiple times or by applying small perturbations to the input. If explanations vary strongly under such small changes, this suggests that the explanation method may be unstable and therefore unreliable in practice.\cite{alvarez-melis_robustness_2018} For example, LIME relies on randomly sampled perturbations to fit a local surrogate model, which can lead to different explanations across runs. SHAP has a deterministic definition, but in many practical settings Shapley values are approximated, which can also introduce variance. In addition, SHAP can behave unpredictably when features are highly correlated, because the attribution can shift between correlated features.\cite{aas_explaining_2020}

However, real-world machine learning systems rarely operate in such a static environment. They are deployed on a continuous stream of data and are retrained regularly. Over time, the distribution of inputs may shift (covariate shift), the class balance may change (prior shift), and the relationship between features and the target may evolve (often referred to as conditional shift or concept drift). In this temporal setting, explanation changes are hard to interpret. \cite{gama_survey_2014, quinonero-candela_dataset_2010} If an explanation for a similar transaction looks different a month later, is that because fraud patterns truly changed, because the model was updated, or because the explanation technique is sensitive to noise?

This dynamic perspective leads to the central challenge of this thesis. On the one hand, explanations should be stable enough to be trusted and used consistently in practice. On the other hand, explanations should not remain artificially constant when the environment, the data, or the model genuinely change. In other words, desirable stability does not mean that explanations should change as little as possible. Rather, explanation changes should be interpretable and proportionate to meaningful changes in the underlying system. This calls for a more structured way to study explanation behavior over time, one that goes beyond static robustness checks and explicitly accounts for evolving data and repeated model retraining.

Despite the wide adoption of SHAP and LIME, there is still limited guidance on how to evaluate their stability in a realistic temporal setting where models are retrained and data distributions evolve. In particular, a naive comparison of explanations over time can easily become an ``apples vs oranges'' comparison, because different models may be evaluated on different instances and under different data conditions. What is needed is a methodology that makes these comparisons more meaningful and helps separate ordinary methodological variability from changes that are associated with temporal evolution in the data and the model.

This thesis addresses this gap by proposing and applying a practical methodology to measure \emph{temporal explanation stability}. The methodology is designed to answer three concrete questions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Is there explanation drift over time?} Do SHAP and LIME explanations change when models are retrained in a rolling-window setting?
  \item \textbf{Is the drift larger than what we would expect from randomness?} We quantify a baseline instability by retraining models multiple times with different seeds and compare temporal drift to this baseline.
  \item \textbf{Does the drift make sense in context?} We relate explanation drift to signals of data drift and to changes in predictive performance, to understand whether explanation changes are consistent with a changing environment.
\end{enumerate}

In summary, this thesis contributes (i) a clear experimental protocol for comparing explanations across time in a way that avoids unfair comparisons, and (ii) an empirical evaluation of SHAP and LIME stability across multiple model classes in different settings. By connecting explanation drift to data drift and performance degradation, we aim to provide a more realistic picture of when these explanation methods can be trusted as models and data evolve over time.











\section{Literature review\label{sec:LitRev}}
\subsection{Explainable Artificial Intelligence}
A universally accepted definition of Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) does not exist. Related terms such as \emph{interpretability}, \emph{explainability}, \emph{transparency}, and \emph{understandability} are used in overlapping but inconsistent ways across the literature \cite{barredo_arrieta_explainable_2020, graziani2022globaltaxonomy, lipton2018mythos}. In broad terms, XAI refers to methods and principles that help human users understand, appropriately trust, and effectively use the outputs of AI systems, especially when those systems are otherwise opaque \cite{nist2021xai, barredo_arrieta_explainable_2020}.

While much of the current popularity of XAI centres on feature attribution techniques, the field is broader. It also encompasses rule-based explanations, counterfactuals, surrogate models, saliency maps, and inherently interpretable model classes \cite{guidotti2018survey, barredo_arrieta_explainable_2020}. This thesis focuses specifically on feature attribution methods, but situating them within this broader landscape is important to avoid equating XAI with feature importance alone.

\subsection{Need for Explainability}
The need for explainability arises because many high-performing machine learning models, such as deep neural networks and ensemble methods, remain difficult for humans to understand. This opacity becomes problematic in high-stakes settings where AI-assisted decisions affect individuals' rights, safety, or access to important opportunities \cite{rudin_stop_2019, barredo_arrieta_explainable_2020}. In such contexts, stakeholders require not only accurate predictions but also insight into how those predictions were produced \cite{doshivelez2017rigorous}.

Explainability is closely related to accountability, oversight, and trust. It enables practitioners to validate model behaviour, identify failure modes, and detect undesirable dependencies or biases in the learned decision logic \cite{guidotti2018survey, mehrabi2021fairness}. In the European regulatory context, the EU AI Act requires high-risk AI systems to be sufficiently transparent that deployers can interpret and appropriately use their outputs \cite{eu_ai_act_2024}. In applied settings, explainability has also been shown to support model debugging and performance improvement \cite{comapuig2021humanintheloopapproachbasedexplainability, rizzi2020ppm}.


\subsection{Interpretability versus Explainability}
Although the terms \emph{interpretability} and \emph{explainability} are often used interchangeably, a useful distinction exists in the literature. \emph{Interpretability} typically refers to the degree to which a model is understandable by design, so-called white-box models, whereas \emph{explainability} refers to post-hoc methods that help humans understand models that are not inherently transparent \cite{lipton2018mythos, graziani2022globaltaxonomy, barredo_arrieta_explainable_2020}. Under this view, linear models, decision trees, and rule-based systems are considered interpretable, while models such as neural networks, non-linear SVMs, and large ensembles typically require external explanation techniques \cite{guidotti2018survey, rudin_stop_2019}.

This distinction matters in practice. Rudin~\cite{rudin_stop_2019} argues that when a task can be solved adequately with an inherently interpretable model, such a model should be preferred over an opaque one explained only after the fact. Nevertheless, black-box models remain widely used because of their flexibility and predictive performance, which sustains the demand for post-hoc XAI methods \cite{barredo_arrieta_explainable_2020, guidotti2018survey}.


\subsection{Types of XAI methods}
The XAI literature contains a broad variety of explanation techniques. Two complementary dimensions are commonly used to organize them \cite{barredo_arrieta_explainable_2020, guidotti2018survey, molnar2022interpretable, verbeke2025aiforbusiness}.

The first dimension concerns \emph{scope}. \emph{Local explanations} focus on a single prediction: why did the model assign this particular score or label to this observation? \emph{Global explanations}, by contrast, describe the model's behaviour at a broader level, identifying which variables systematically drive predictions across the data distribution \cite{molnar2022interpretable}. The boundary is not always strict; some methods generate instance-level explanations that can be aggregated to obtain global insights \cite{guidotti2018survey}.

The second dimension concerns \emph{model dependence}. \emph{Model-agnostic} methods treat the predictive model as a black box and operate solely on the input--output relationship, which makes them applicable across model classes \cite{molnar2022interpretable}. \emph{Model-specific} methods exploit the internal structure of a particular model family, often gaining computational efficiency at the cost of generality \cite{guidotti2018survey}. For example, coefficient-based interpretation in linear regression is model-specific, whereas permutation-based feature importance is model-agnostic \cite{molnar2022interpretable, verbeke2025aiforbusiness}.

Within this taxonomy, several families of explanation methods exist, including feature attribution methods, surrogate methods, rule-based and example-based explanations, counterfactual explanations, and visual explanation techniques such as saliency maps \cite{barredo_arrieta_explainable_2020, guidotti2018survey}. Because this thesis studies tabular predictive models, the most relevant family is \emph{feature attribution}: methods that quantify how much each input variable contributed to a prediction. The two most prominent feature attribution methods are LIME and SHAP, which are discussed in the following subsections.


\emph{Model-specific} methods, by contrast, exploit structural properties of a particular model family. This often allows them to produce explanations that are computationally efficient or closely aligned with the model formulation, but at the cost of reduced generality \cite{guidotti2018survey,molnar2022interpretable}. Examples include coefficient-based interpretations for linear and logistic regression, impurity-based feature importance in tree ensembles, and methods such as Grad-CAM for convolutional neural networks \cite{selvaraju2017grad,verbeke2025aiforbusiness}.

These two dimensions are complementary. A method can be local and model-agnostic, such as LIME, or global and model-specific, such as regression scorecards. Other methods, such as SHAP, can be both local and global depending on how they are computed and summarized \cite{lundberg_unified_2017,verbeke2025aiforbusiness}.

\subsection{LIME}
Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations (LIME) is one of the most widely used post-hoc XAI methods for tabular data \cite{ribeiro_why_2016}. It belongs to the family of local surrogate methods: rather than explaining the black-box model globally, LIME approximates its behaviour in a small neighbourhood around the instance of interest using a simpler, interpretable model such as a sparse linear regression \cite{ribeiro_why_2016, molnar2022interpretable}.

Concretely, LIME proceeds in four steps. First, it generates perturbed versions of the instance to be explained by sampling around that instance's feature values. Second, it queries the black-box model for predictions on each perturbed observation. Third, it weights these observations by proximity to the original instance using a locality kernel. Fourth, it fits a sparse interpretable model to this weighted dataset; the resulting coefficients serve as the explanation \cite{ribeiro_why_2016, verbeke2025aiforbusiness}. For tabular data, the perturbation distribution is typically constructed from statistics of a reference dataset, which means the explanation depends not only on the instance being explained but also on the reference data used to define the neighbourhood \cite{ribeiro_why_2016, molnar2022interpretable}.

This procedural flexibility is both a strength and a limitation. LIME's design choices, the kernel width, the distance metric, the perturbation strategy, the surrogate model class, and the number of features retained, are not uniquely determined by theory and must be set by the user \cite{ribeiro_why_2016, molnar2022interpretable}. Different settings can yield different explanations for the same prediction, even when the underlying model has not changed. Moreover, if the black-box model is highly non-linear within the local neighbourhood, the linear surrogate may provide an incomplete or misleading approximation \cite{guidotti2018survey}. These sensitivities are a recurring theme in the stability literature and are directly relevant to this thesis.


\subsection{SHAP}
SHAP (\emph{SHapley Additive exPlanations}) is a feature attribution framework grounded in cooperative game theory \cite{lundberg_unified_2017}. It explains a prediction by treating input features as players in a coalition and distributing the prediction among them according to their marginal contributions. Concretely, SHAP decomposes a prediction into additive feature contributions relative to a baseline, typically the average prediction over a background dataset:
\[
f(x) = \phi_0 + \sum_{j=1}^{p} \phi_j,
\]
where $\phi_0$ is the baseline and $\phi_j$ is the SHAP value of feature~$j$ for observation~$x$ \cite{lundberg_unified_2017, molnar2022interpretable}. A positive value indicates that the feature pushes the prediction above the baseline; a negative value indicates the opposite.

Lundberg and Lee~\cite{lundberg_unified_2017} show that, within the class of additive feature attribution methods, SHAP values are the unique solution satisfying three desirable properties: local accuracy, missingness, and consistency. This theoretical grounding distinguishes SHAP from heuristic attribution methods. In addition, because SHAP produces one value per feature per instance, local attributions can be aggregated across observations to yield global importance rankings, summary plots, and dependence plots \cite{lundberg2020trees}.

Exact Shapley value computation is combinatorially expensive, so practical implementations rely on approximations tailored to specific model families. \emph{KernelSHAP} is the most general variant: it estimates SHAP values through a weighted local regression and can be applied to any model, but at considerable computational cost \cite{lundberg_unified_2017}. For tree-based models, \emph{TreeSHAP} exploits the recursive tree structure to compute Shapley values efficiently \cite{lundberg2020trees}. TreeSHAP is often described as exact, but this requires qualification: it computes exact Shapley values with respect to a particular feature-removal strategy (typically the interventional or path-dependent approach), which is not necessarily equivalent to the conditional Shapley values one might conceptually prefer \cite{lundberg2020trees, aas_explaining_2020}. The choice of SHAP variant therefore affects not only computation time but also the meaning of the resulting attributions.

More broadly, SHAP values depend on several choices beyond the model itself: the explainer variant, the background dataset against which predictions are contrasted, and the assumptions about how withheld features are handled \cite{molnar2022interpretable, lundberg_unified_2017}. When features are strongly correlated, attribution becomes particularly sensitive, because predictive information shared among correlated variables can be distributed across them in unstable ways \cite{aas_explaining_2020}. These dependencies are important context for studying explanation stability, which is the focus of this thesis.


\subsection{Variability in SHAP and LIME explanations}
\label{subsec:variability}
The preceding sections described how both LIME and SHAP depend on design choices and approximation strategies beyond the predictive model itself. This section synthesizes these sources of variability and introduces a distinction that is central to the rest of this thesis: the difference between \emph{explainer-induced} variability and \emph{model-induced} variability.

Explainer-induced variability refers to explanation changes that arise from the explanation procedure while the underlying model remains fixed. For LIME, the main sources are the stochastic perturbation sampling, the kernel width, and the reference data used to define the neighbourhood. Recent theoretical work by Tan et al.~\cite{tan_glime_2023} has shown that LIME is particularly sensitive to the interaction between its weighting function, regularization, and sampling distribution, and that these interactions can substantially affect explanation stability. For SHAP, the main sources depend on the variant used. KernelSHAP, being sampling-based, is subject to estimation variance that depends on sampling budget and convergence behaviour \cite{covert_improving_2021}. TreeSHAP, by contrast, is deterministic for a given model and background set, but explanations still depend on the choice of background data (used to integrate out missing features). Yuan et al.~\cite{yuan_empirical_2022} show empirically that both local SHAP values and aggregated feature rankings become more stable as the background dataset grows larger and more representative.

Model-induced variability, by contrast, arises when the prediction function itself changes. Since SHAP and LIME are post-hoc methods applied to a fitted model, any change in the model propagates to the explanation. Stochastic training procedures, different random seeds, bootstrap samples, or altered hyperparameters can all produce a different fitted function and therefore different explanations, even when the explainer configuration is held constant \cite{ribeiro_why_2016, lundberg_unified_2017}. In a static setting where the model is trained once, this source of variability is often ignored. In a dynamic setting where models are retrained periodically, it becomes unavoidable and potentially dominant.

This distinction matters because observed explanation changes in practice are typically a mixture of both sources. When an explanation looks different from one model version to the next, it may reflect a genuine change in the model's learned behaviour, sensitivity of the explainer to its own randomness, or both. Disentangling these sources is a prerequisite for meaningful stability assessment, and it is one of the central objectives of the methodology proposed in this thesis.

\subsection{Stability as a quality criterion for explainable AI}
\label{subsec:stability}
If explanations are to support trust and decision-making, they should be reasonably stable: similar instances should not receive fundamentally different explanations without a meaningful reason \cite{alvarez-melis_robustness_2018, bhatt2020evaluating}. When small input changes produce large explanation changes, users may perceive the explanation as arbitrary, which undermines its practical value. Stability is therefore widely regarded as an important quality criterion for XAI, alongside faithfulness and interpretability \cite{bhatt2020evaluating, longo2024xai2}.

The existing stability literature focuses almost exclusively on \emph{static} settings, where the data distribution and the trained model are assumed fixed. In this setting, stability is typically assessed by applying small perturbations to an input and measuring how strongly the explanation changes \cite{alvarez-melis_robustness_2018, bhatt2020evaluating}. The central finding is that many post-hoc methods are more sensitive than desirable. Alvarez-Melis and Jaakkola~\cite{alvarez-melis_robustness_2018} formalize this as a local Lipschitz continuity requirement on the explanation function and show empirically that several methods violate it: small input perturbations can produce disproportionately large explanation changes. Bhatt et al.~\cite{bhatt2020evaluating} reach a complementary conclusion, arguing that explanation methods should be evaluated on sensitivity alongside faithfulness, and that low sensitivity is a necessary condition for trustworthy attributions. These concerns are echoed in recent meta-surveys that identify robustness and stability as persistent open challenges in XAI evaluation \cite{saeed2023metasurvey, longo2024xai2}.

These static findings establish that explanation instability is a real and measurable problem. However, they leave open a question that is directly relevant to deployed systems: what does stability mean when the model itself changes over time? The next section addresses this transition from static to dynamic settings.


\subsection{From static to dynamic settings}
Real-world machine learning systems rarely operate in the static regime assumed by most stability research. Input distributions shift, class balances change, and the relationship between features and the target may evolve; phenomena collectively referred to as data drift \cite{gama2014survey, bayram2022concept}. To maintain predictive performance, models are periodically retrained on more recent data.

This has a direct implication for explainability. In a dynamic setting, a change in explanation is not necessarily a defect. If the data-generating process has shifted and the model has been retrained to reflect new patterns, then a different explanation may represent a meaningful adaptation rather than methodological instability. The challenge is therefore not to determine \emph{whether} explanations change, but \emph{why} they change.

This challenge is difficult because temporal explanation differences confound multiple sources of variation simultaneously. Observed changes may arise because the model has learned different relationships from new data, because stochastic training elements (random seeds, bootstrap samples, hyperparameter selection) produced a different fitted model, or because the explainer itself is sensitive to its own configuration (the sources of explainer-induced and model-induced variability discussed in Section~\ref{subsec:variability}). In a static setting, these sources can be isolated through controlled perturbation experiments. In a dynamic setting, they are intertwined.

Recent work has begun to connect explainability with non-stationary learning environments, though from a different angle than this thesis. Several studies use feature relevance analysis as a tool to \emph{detect or describe} concept drift rather than to study the stability of explanations themselves \cite{hinder2023model, siirtola2023feature}. Pelosi et al.~\cite{pelosi2025explainability} provide a systematic review of explainability in the presence of drift, confirming that the area is active but that stability under retraining has received limited direct attention. Surveys on XAI for temporal data similarly note that robustness and stability remain insufficiently addressed \cite{rojat2021xaits}.

This thesis addresses the resulting gap. Existing static stability research (Section~\ref{subsec:stability}) establishes that explanation instability is a measurable problem; the concept drift literature establishes that models and data change over time; but the question of how to assess explanation stability \emph{across} model retraining cycles in a principled way remains open. The methodology proposed in the next chapter provides a framework for answering this question by explicitly separating retraining-induced variability from temporal explanation drift, and by contextualizing explanation changes with data drift and predictive performance.

\section{Methodology: Temporal Stability of XAI Under Rolling Retraining}
\label{sec:methodology}

This section presents a general framework for evaluating the temporal stability
of feature-attribution explanations in machine learning systems that are
periodically retrained. The framework is designed for supervised classification
settings in which a model is deployed on a continuous stream of data and
retrained at regular intervals on recent observations. It is agnostic to the
specific model family and to the specific attribution method; concrete choices
made in our empirical study are deferred to Section~\ref{sec:implementation}.

The core idea is straightforward. When a model is retrained, its explanations
may change. Some of that change may reflect genuine shifts in the data or in
the model's learned behaviour, while other changes may simply be artefacts of
retraining randomness or of the explanation method itself. To assess explanation
stability in a meaningful way, one needs to separate these sources. The
framework achieves this through four components.

First, a \textbf{rolling retraining setup} defines how successive model
versions are trained on time-ordered windows and how they are compared on a
shared set of future instances. 
This shared evaluation slice ensures that observed explanation differences are
not caused by comparing different instances. Instead, they reflect changes in
the fitted model versions and, where applicable, in the associated preprocessing
and explanation pipeline.

Second, \textbf{explanation drift measurement} quantifies how much explanations
change between adjacent model versions on an operationally relevant subset of
the shared evaluation slice. For each instance in this subset, the attributions
produced by the older and newer model versions are compared using distance
measures that capture complementary aspects of explanation similarity.
Aggregating these instance-level distances yields a summary of local explanation
drift for each pair of model versions.

Third, a \textbf{baseline instability estimate} captures the explanation
variability that arises from retraining randomness within the same temporal
window. By training multiple model replicas on resampled versions of the same
window, using different random seeds, the framework estimates how much
explanations can vary even when the temporal training period is held fixed. This
baseline serves as a reference against which explanation changes between
successive retraining windows can be judged.

Fourth, \textbf{contextualisation} relates explanation drift to observable
changes in the data and in predictive performance. If explanation drift
coincides with measurable covariate shift, changes in the target distribution,
or performance degradation, the drift is more likely to reflect a meaningful
change in the modelling problem. If explanation drift occurs in the absence of
such signals, it may indicate instability in the model or the explanation
method. By presenting these quantities together over time, the framework
supports a diagnostic interpretation of when and why explanations change.

The remainder of this section formalises each component in turn.


\subsection{Rolling Retraining Setup}
\label{sec:rolling_setup}

Let the observed data be partitioned into chronologically ordered subsets
\[
D_{t_1}, D_{t_2}, \dots, D_{t_K},
\]
where each $D_{t_k}$ contains all instances observed during time step $t_k$.
Each instance consists of a feature vector $x \in \mathbb{R}^p$ and a target
label $y$. The granularity of a time step is application-dependent and should
reflect the natural cadence of the deployment environment.

\paragraph{Training windows.}
For a fixed window length $L$, the training window ending at time step $t_k$ is
defined as
\[
\mathcal{W}_k = \bigcup_{j=k-L+1}^{k} D_{t_j}, \quad k \geq L.
\]
Successive model versions are obtained by advancing the window by $s$ time
steps. A pairwise comparison involves two model versions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Model $A$, trained on $\mathcal{W}_k$,
    \item Model $B$, trained on $\mathcal{W}_{k+s}$.
\end{itemize}
The window length $L$ is kept fixed throughout the comparison, while the window
itself moves forward in time.

\paragraph{Common evaluation slice.}
To ensure a fair comparison between model versions, both are evaluated on the
same set of future instances. For a pair $(A, B)$, the common evaluation slice
is defined as
\[
\mathcal{E}_{A,B} = \bigcup_{j=k+s+1}^{k+s+h} D_{t_j}, \quad k + s + h \leq K,
\]
where $h$ is the length of the evaluation horizon. Because this slice lies
chronologically after both training windows, neither model has seen any of
these instances during training. This design is essential: without a shared
evaluation set, observed explanation differences would confound model
differences with differences in the instances being explained.

\paragraph{Replica training.}
Within each training window, the framework trains $R$ model replicas.
Hyperparameters are selected once per window using a time-aware validation
procedure that respects temporal ordering. The replicas within that window then
share the same hyperparameters, but differ through controlled sources of
randomness:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item a different random seed for the learning algorithm;
    \item a bootstrap sample of the training data from the same window.
\end{enumerate}
If class imbalance is present, class-stratified bootstrapping should be used to
preserve the original class balance while still introducing variation across
replicas. For window $\mathcal{W}_k$, this yields replicas
$f_A^{(1)}, \dots, f_A^{(R)}$, and similarly
$f_B^{(1)}, \dots, f_B^{(R)}$ for window $\mathcal{W}_{k+s}$.

The purpose of training multiple replicas is not to produce an ensemble for
deployment. Rather, replicas serve as a tool to estimate the explanation
variability that arises from retraining randomness alone. By observing how much
explanations differ across replicas trained on the same temporal window, we
obtain a baseline against which temporal explanation drift can be compared.

The number of replicas $R$, the window length $L$, the step size $s$, the
evaluation horizon $h$, and the specific tuning protocol are application-
dependent. The values used in the empirical study, along with the concrete model
families and tuning details, are described in Section~\ref{sec:implementation}.


\subsection{Preprocessing and the Reference Frame}
\label{subsec:reference_frame}

Preprocessing has to be treated carefully in a rolling retraining setting,
because it can affect both the trained model and the diagnostic quantities used
to compare model versions. We distinguish between preprocessing as part of the
predictive pipeline and preprocessing as part of the evaluation framework.

For model training, each model version is treated as a complete predictive
pipeline. This means that transformations such as scaling, imputation, or
encoding may be fitted separately for each training window, as would be the case
in a realistic retraining scenario. The only requirement is that these
transformations are fitted using the corresponding training window only, so that
no future information leaks into the model.

For covariate drift measurement, however, the two training windows must be
represented in a common reference frame. If each window were scaled separately,
changes in feature location and spread could be normalised away, even though
such changes are precisely what the drift metric is intended to capture. We
therefore compute covariate drift after expressing both windows in a shared
reference frame. In practice, this can be done by fitting a reference
transformation on a fixed window and applying it to both windows before
computing feature-wise distances. Features that already have a common scale,
such as binary indicators, can be left unchanged.

For explanation comparisons, the issue is slightly different. The goal is not
to remove every effect of retraining, but to evaluate how explanations would
change in an operational retraining setup. We therefore treat the fitted
preprocessing pipeline and, where applicable, the explainer reference data as
part of the model version being explained. In other words, explanations are
compared for the same evaluation instances and the same feature identities, but
each model version may be explained using the preprocessing and reference data
associated with that retrained version.

This choice has an important implication. The resulting explanation drift
should be interpreted as \emph{end-to-end explanation drift}: it captures changes
in the trained predictive model together with changes induced by the
preprocessing and explanation procedure used for that model version. This is
appropriate for the present study, because in a real deployment the explanation
procedure would typically be updated together with the retrained model. It also
means that the drift metrics should not be interpreted as isolating only the
effect of changed model parameters.

For SHAP-based explanations, for example, the background distribution affects
the expected value relative to which feature contributions are computed. Changing
the background can therefore alter the attribution vector, even for the same
instance. Similarly, for LIME, the reference data used to generate perturbations
influences the local surrogate model. The use of cosine distance and rank-based
overlap reduces the emphasis on absolute attribution magnitudes, but it does
not make the comparison completely independent of these reference choices.
Consequently, the attribution distances reported in this thesis are interpreted
as diagnostics of operational explanation stability, rather than as pure
model-parameter distances.


\subsection{Contextualizing Explanation Drift With Data Drift and Performance}
\label{subsec:contextualize}

Explanation drift is not necessarily undesirable. If the data distribution
changes over time, a retrained model may legitimately rely on different features
or use the same features in different ways. We therefore interpret explanation
drift together with covariate drift, target drift, and predictive performance.

\paragraph{Covariate drift.}
Covariate drift measures how much the feature distribution changes between the
two training windows. We compute a univariate Wasserstein distance for each
feature and average these distances across features. Before computing distances,
both windows are expressed in a common reference frame, as discussed in
Section~\ref{subsec:reference_frame}. Denoting the transformed feature
distributions in training windows $\mathcal{W}_A$ and $\mathcal{W}_B$ for
feature $j$ by $X_A^{(j)}$ and $X_B^{(j)}$, we define
\[
\Delta_X(A,B)
=
\frac{1}{p}
\sum_{j=1}^{p}
W\!\left(X_A^{(j)}, X_B^{(j)}\right),
\]
where $W(\cdot,\cdot)$ is the Wasserstein distance.

This scalar should be interpreted as a compact summary of average covariate
movement across the feature set. Since the feature-wise distances are
nonnegative, changes do not cancel each other out. However, averaging can dilute
strong drift in a small number of features. For that reason, \(\Delta_X\) is
used as a contextual signal rather than as a complete description of all
distributional change.

\paragraph{Target drift.}
Target drift is measured as the absolute difference in positive-class rates
between the two training windows:
\[
\Delta_Y(A,B)
=
\left|
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{W}_A|}
\sum_{(x,y)\in\mathcal{W}_A} y
-
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{W}_B|}
\sum_{(x,y)\in\mathcal{W}_B} y
\right|.
\]
For binary classification, this captures changes in the class balance over
time. In other supervised settings, an analogous target-distribution summary
should be chosen.

\paragraph{Performance change.}
Predictive performance is evaluated on the full common evaluation slice
\(\mathcal{E}_{A,B}\). Let \(M(f,\mathcal{E}_{A,B})\) denote an appropriate
performance score for the task, chosen such that higher values indicate better
performance. The performance change between model versions is then defined as
\[
\Delta_{\mathrm{perf}}(A,B)
=
M(f_B,\mathcal{E}_{A,B})
-
M(f_A,\mathcal{E}_{A,B}).
\]
A positive value indicates that the newer model \(B\) performs better than the
older model \(A\) on the shared evaluation slice, while a negative value
indicates a performance decrease.

Interpreting \(\Delta_{\mathrm{perf}}\) together with \(\Delta_X\),
\(\Delta_Y\), and the explanation drift metrics helps distinguish between
potentially meaningful adaptation and undesirable instability. For example,
large explanation drift accompanied by covariate or target drift may indicate
that the model has adapted to a changing environment. In contrast, large
explanation drift in the absence of observable data drift or performance
improvement may suggest instability in the model or explanation procedure.


\subsection{Predictions and Attributions}
\label{subsec:predictions_attributions}

\paragraph{Prediction scores and the explained subset.}
For each pair of model versions, the replicas of a given version produce
predicted scores on the common evaluation slice \(\mathcal{E}_{A,B}\). These
scores are averaged across replicas to obtain a stable ranking of evaluation
instances:
\[
\hat{p}_A(x)
=
\frac{1}{R}
\sum_{r=1}^{R}
f_A^{(r)}(x),
\qquad
\hat{p}_B(x)
=
\frac{1}{R}
\sum_{r=1}^{R}
f_B^{(r)}(x).
\]
In the binary classification setting considered in the empirical study, these
scores are predicted positive-class probabilities. The averaged predictions are
used to define the subset of instances for which explanations are compared.

In many operational settings, explanations are mainly inspected for instances
that are considered relevant by the model, such as high-risk transactions,
customers, or claims. We therefore restrict the explanation analysis to a
flagged subset of the common evaluation slice. For binary classification, this
subset is defined using the combined relevance score
\[
s_{A,B}(x)
=
\max\!\left(\hat{p}_A(x), \hat{p}_B(x)\right).
\]
The flagged set \(\mathcal{F}_{A,B}\) then consists of the top \(k\) fraction of
evaluation instances ranked by \(s_{A,B}(x)\):
\[
\mathcal{F}_{A,B}
=
\operatorname{top}_{\lceil k|\mathcal{E}_{A,B}|\rceil}
\left\{
s_{A,B}(x)
:
x \in \mathcal{E}_{A,B}
\right\}.
\]

This construction has two advantages. First, it ensures that both model versions
are explained on exactly the same instances, which is necessary for an
instance-wise comparison of their attributions. Second, the use of the maximum
makes the selection symmetric with respect to the two model versions. An
instance is eligible for explanation if either the older model or the newer
model assigns it a high score. This includes both instances that remain
high-risk under both models and instances whose assessed risk changes strongly
after retraining.

The flagged set should therefore not be interpreted as the set of instances
that both models would necessarily act upon. Rather, it is a fixed comparison
set for a given pair, designed to capture the cases that are operationally
relevant to at least one of the two model versions. If the evaluation-slice size
varies over time, the absolute number of flagged instances may also vary; the
parameter \(k\) fixes the fraction of the evaluation slice that is explained.

For tasks where no natural risk score exists, the same idea can be adapted by
choosing a task-specific relevance criterion. In a regression setting, for
example, one could select instances with the largest predicted values, largest
predicted changes, or highest expected operational impact. If no such criterion
is meaningful, explanation stability can instead be evaluated on a representative
or stratified subsample of the evaluation slice.

\paragraph{Attribution vectors.}
For a trained replica \(f^{(r)}\) and an instance \(x\), let
\[
\phi\!\left(f^{(r)}, x\right) \in \mathbb{R}^p
\]
denote the attribution vector returned by the chosen explanation method, where
\(\phi_j\) is the attribution assigned to feature \(j\). 
For binary classification, the attribution vector refers to the model output
associated with the positive class. Depending on the explanation method and
model family, this may be a probability, a raw margin, or a logit. For
regression, it refers to the scalar prediction output.

The main object of interest in this thesis is the temporal stability of
post-hoc feature-attribution methods such as SHAP and LIME. These methods
produce local, instance-level explanations and are therefore evaluated on the
flagged set \(\mathcal{F}_{A,B}\).

As an additional reference point, the empirical study also includes a
transparent linear model. In the binary classification setting considered here,
this is logistic regression; in a regression setting, the analogous choice would
be linear regression. For such models, the fitted coefficient vector provides a
direct model-level summary of feature effects. We therefore analyse coefficient
stability directly, rather than treating logistic regression as a post-hoc local
explanation target. Replica-based coefficient comparisons are used to estimate
within-window sensitivity to resampling, while full-window coefficient vectors
can be compared visually across model versions. This coefficient analysis is a
transparent-model reference, not a post-hoc local explanation method.

\paragraph{SHAP attributions.}
For SHAP-based explanations, attributions are computed per replica and per
explained instance. When computationally feasible, SHAP is computed for every
instance in the flagged set \(\mathcal{F}_{A,B}\), producing attribution arrays
of shape
\[
(R, |\mathcal{F}_{A,B}|, p).
\]
The exact SHAP variant depends on the model class. Model-specific variants such
as TreeSHAP can be substantially more efficient than model-agnostic or
gradient-based variants. For this reason, the decision to explain the full
flagged set or a subsample is treated as an implementation choice.
When a SHAP subsample is used, the same subsample is used for both model
versions and all replicas within the corresponding window pair, so that
attribution distances remain instance-wise comparable.

\paragraph{LIME attributions.}
LIME constructs a local surrogate model separately for each explained instance.
Because this makes the method computationally demanding when many instances and
replicas are involved, the framework allows LIME to be evaluated on a subsample
of the flagged set:
\[
\mathcal{F}^{\mathrm{LIME}}_{A,B}
\subseteq
\mathcal{F}_{A,B},
\qquad
|\mathcal{F}^{\mathrm{LIME}}_{A,B}|
=
\min\!\left(n_{\mathrm{sub}}^{\mathrm{LIME}},
|\mathcal{F}_{A,B}|\right).
\]
The same LIME subsample is used for both model versions and all replicas within
a pair, so that attribution differences are computed instance-wise.

To make this subsample representative of the flagged set, instances are first
ordered by the combined relevance score \(s_{A,B}(x)\). The ordered list is then
sampled approximately evenly across its full range. This avoids selecting only
the most extreme flagged instances and ensures that the LIME analysis covers
both very high-scoring and less extreme flagged cases.

Finally, when binary indicator features are present, they are treated as
categorical features in LIME. Without this adjustment, LIME's continuous
perturbation mechanism could generate fractional values for indicators that are
only meaningful as \(0\) or \(1\).


\subsection{Distance Measures for Attribution Vectors}
\label{subsec:distance_measures}

The local drift and baseline-instability measurements used in this framework
ultimately require comparing pairs of attribution vectors
\(u, v \in \mathbb{R}^p\). We use two complementary distance measures: cosine
distance and rank-biased-overlap distance.

\paragraph{Cosine distance.}
Cosine distance compares the direction of two attribution vectors:
\[
d_{\mathrm{cos}}(u,v)
=
1
-
\frac{u \cdot v}{\|u\|\,\|v\|}.
\]
This distance is low when two explanations assign attribution to features in a
similar relative pattern, including the same sign pattern. It is less sensitive
to overall differences in attribution magnitude, because multiplying all
attributions in one vector by a positive constant does not change the cosine
distance.

Cosine distance is therefore useful when the question is whether two
explanations rely on the same features in broadly the same way, rather than
whether the absolute attribution values are identical.

\paragraph{Rank-biased-overlap distance.}
The second distance compares the ranking of features induced by the attribution
vectors. Features are ranked by decreasing absolute attribution magnitude, so
that the highest-ranked features are those with the largest contribution in
absolute value. The resulting rankings are compared using Rank-Biased Overlap
(RBO), which gives more weight to agreement near the top of the ranking:
\[
d_{\mathrm{rbo}}(u,v)
=
1
-
\mathrm{RBO}\!\left(
\operatorname{rank}(|u|),
\operatorname{rank}(|v|);
\rho
\right),
\]
where \(\rho \in (0,1)\) is the persistence parameter. A smaller value of
\(\rho\) makes the comparison more top-heavy, while a value closer to \(1\)
places relatively more weight on agreement deeper in the ranking.

This distance is useful when the main question is whether the same features are
identified as most important, regardless of the exact attribution magnitudes.

\paragraph{Edge cases.}
If both attribution vectors are numerically zero, the two explanations are
treated as identical and the distance is set to zero. If exactly one of the two
vectors is numerically zero, the comparison is undefined: there is no meaningful
direction or feature ranking for the zero vector. Such distances are marked as
missing and excluded from subsequent aggregation.

These cases are mainly included for numerical robustness. In normal operation,
the interpretation of the results is driven by the nonzero attribution vectors.

Together, cosine distance and RBO distance capture different aspects of
explanation stability. Cosine distance is sensitive to the relative signed
pattern of attributions, while RBO distance focuses only on the ordering of the
most important features. Reporting both helps distinguish changes in attribution
patterns from changes in the identity of the top-ranked features.

\subsection{Dynamic Cross-Window Drift}
\label{subsec:dynamic_cross_window}

The primary temporal drift measure compares explanations produced by two
successive model versions on the same explained instances. Let
\(\mathcal{S}_{A,B}^{(e)}\) denote the set of instances explained by explainer
\(e\) for the pair \((A,B)\). For SHAP, this is typically the full flagged set
\(\mathcal{F}_{A,B}\). For LIME, it may be a subsample of the flagged set for
computational reasons.

This local dynamic-drift definition is applied to post-hoc explainer streams
such as SHAP and LIME. The logistic-regression coefficient reference is handled
separately, because its coefficients are global model parameters rather than
instance-level local attributions.

For a given instance \(x \in \mathcal{S}_{A,B}^{(e)}\), dynamic drift between
model versions \(A\) and \(B\) under distance \(d\) is computed over all
cross-window replica pairs:
\[
\delta_{\mathrm{dyn}}(x; e, d)
=
\operatorname{nanmean}_{1 \le r,s \le R}
d\!\left(
\phi_e\!\left(f_A^{(r)}, x\right),
\phi_e\!\left(f_B^{(s)}, x\right)
\right),
\]
where \(d \in \{d_{\mathrm{cos}}, d_{\mathrm{rbo}}\}\), and
\(\phi_e\) denotes the attribution vector produced by explainer \(e\).
Distances that are undefined because of zero-vector edge cases are ignored in
the nan-robust average.

This cross-replica formulation is deliberate. Averaging attributions before
computing a distance would collapse each window into a single representative
explanation vector. By instead computing distances between all pairs of
replicas, the measure preserves the variability induced by retraining and
captures the explanation behaviour of the full replica population.

The local explanation drift for the pair \((A,B)\) is then obtained by
aggregating over the explained instances:
\[
\Delta_E^{\mathrm{loc}}(A,B; e, d)
=
\operatorname{nanmedian}_{x \in \mathcal{S}_{A,B}^{(e)}}
\delta_{\mathrm{dyn}}(x; e, d).
\]
The median is used because attribution distances can occasionally contain
outlying or undefined values. A nan-robust median excludes undefined distances
without allowing them to dominate the aggregate.


\subsection{Within-Window Baseline Instability}
\label{subsec:baseline_eval}

Dynamic explanation drift is only meaningful when interpreted relative to the
amount of explanation variability that arises even without moving forward in
time. We therefore compute a within-window baseline instability separately for
each of the two model versions.

For a given instance \(x\), the within-window baseline instability of model
version \(A\) under explainer \(e\) and distance \(d\) is defined as the median
pairwise distance between distinct replicas trained on the same temporal
window:
\[
\delta_A^{\mathrm{base}}(x; e, d)
=
\operatorname{nanmedian}_{1 \le r < r' \le R}
d\!\left(
\phi_e\!\left(f_A^{(r)}, x\right),
\phi_e\!\left(f_A^{(r')}, x\right)
\right).
\]
The same quantity is computed for model version \(B\):
\[
\delta_B^{\mathrm{base}}(x; e, d)
=
\operatorname{nanmedian}_{1 \le r < r' \le R}
d\!\left(
\phi_e\!\left(f_B^{(r)}, x\right),
\phi_e\!\left(f_B^{(r')}, x\right)
\right).
\]

These instance-level baseline values are then aggregated over the same
explained set used for the dynamic drift calculation:
\[
\sigma_E(A; e, d)
=
\operatorname{nanmedian}_{x \in \mathcal{S}_{A,B}^{(e)}}
\delta_A^{\mathrm{base}}(x; e, d),
\]
\[
\sigma_E(B; e, d)
=
\operatorname{nanmedian}_{x \in \mathcal{S}_{A,B}^{(e)}}
\delta_B^{\mathrm{base}}(x; e, d).
\]

The two baseline values are reported separately. This is more informative than
collapsing them into a single pooled quantity, because the older and newer
training windows may have different levels of retraining-induced explanation
instability. For example, a later window may contain a more heterogeneous data
distribution, making its replicas less stable even before any cross-window
comparison is made.

Conceptually, the baseline captures variability caused by the retraining
procedure within a fixed temporal window. This includes variation due to
bootstrap sampling, random seeds in the learning algorithm, and, for post-hoc
explainers, any randomness introduced when the explanations are generated.

For the transparent reference model, the analogous baseline is
computed directly on the replica coefficient vectors within a window. Since
these coefficients are global model parameters rather than local explanations,
there is no aggregation over explained instances for this reference analysis.

\subsection{Global Attribution Drift}
\label{subsec:global_drift}

In addition to local explanation drift, we compute a global attribution-drift
measure for SHAP. For each explained instance \(x\), SHAP values are first
averaged across replicas:
\[
\bar{\phi}_A(x)
=
\frac{1}{R}
\sum_{r=1}^{R}
\phi_{\mathrm{SHAP}}\!\left(f_A^{(r)}, x\right),
\qquad
\bar{\phi}_B(x)
=
\frac{1}{R}
\sum_{r=1}^{R}
\phi_{\mathrm{SHAP}}\!\left(f_B^{(r)}, x\right).
\]
The global importance of feature \(j\) is then defined as the mean absolute
replica-averaged SHAP value over the explained set:
\[
g_A(j)
=
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{S}_{A,B}^{(\mathrm{SHAP})}|}
\sum_{x \in \mathcal{S}_{A,B}^{(\mathrm{SHAP})}}
\left|\bar{\phi}_{A,j}(x)\right|,
\]
\[
g_B(j)
=
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{S}_{A,B}^{(\mathrm{SHAP})}|}
\sum_{x \in \mathcal{S}_{A,B}^{(\mathrm{SHAP})}}
\left|\bar{\phi}_{B,j}(x)\right|.
\]
Features are ranked by these global importance scores, and global drift is
defined as
\[
\Delta_E^{\mathrm{glob}}(A,B)
=
1
-
\mathrm{RBO}\!\left(
\operatorname{rank}(g_A),
\operatorname{rank}(g_B);
\rho
\right),
\]
using the same persistence parameter \(\rho\) as in the local RBO distance.

This measure captures whether the overall ranking of important features changes
between two adjacent model versions. We restrict this global drift measure to
SHAP because mean absolute SHAP values are a standard way of summarising local
SHAP attributions into global feature importances. LIME, by contrast, fits a
separate local surrogate model around each explained instance. Aggregating such
local surrogate coefficients into a global ranking is less directly tied to the
method's intended interpretation and is therefore left out of the global drift
analysis.


\subsection{Explainer Stochasticity Diagnostic}
\label{subsec:explainer_instability}

Post-hoc explainers can introduce variability even when the trained model and
the explained instances are held fixed. To measure this source of variation
separately, we include an explainer stochasticity diagnostic. It acts as a reference.
Together with the baselines and data drift measurements, 
it should provide context for interpreting the dynamic explanation drift.

For each window pair, explainer, and side \(A\) or \(B\), we select a
representative replica. In the implementation, this is the replica whose
predictions on the explained instances are closest to the replica-averaged
prediction. Holding this trained model, the explained instances, the feature
order, and the explainer configuration fixed, we run the explainer multiple
times using different explainer random seeds where applicable.

Let \(\phi_e^{(q)}(x)\) denote the attribution vector produced by repeated
explainer run \(q\) for instance \(x\). For each side, explainer stochasticity
under distance \(d\) is measured as
\[
\eta(A; e, d)
=
\operatorname{nanmedian}_{x}
\operatorname{nanmedian}_{q<q'}
d\!\left(
\phi_{e,A}^{(q)}(x),
\phi_{e,A}^{(q')}(x)
\right),
\]
and analogously for \(\eta(B; e, d)\), with
\(d \in \{d_{\mathrm{cos}}, d_{\mathrm{rbo}}\}\).


\subsection{Reporting Across Time}
\label{subsec:reporting_across_time}

The full procedure is repeated for all feasible adjacent window pairs along the
timeline. For each pair, and for each post-hoc model--explainer combination under study,
we report:
\begin{itemize}
    \item covariate drift \(\Delta_X\);
    \item target drift \(\Delta_Y\);
    \item predictive performance of both model versions and the performance
          change \(\Delta_{\mathrm{perf}}\);
    \item local dynamic explanation drift \(\Delta_E^{\mathrm{loc}}\) under
          cosine and RBO distance;
    \item within-window baseline instability for both model versions,
          \(\sigma_E(A)\) and \(\sigma_E(B)\), under cosine and RBO distance;
    \item global attribution drift \(\Delta_E^{\mathrm{glob}}\) for SHAP;
    \item the explainer stochasticity diagnostic.
\end{itemize}

Together, these quantities form a temporal diagnostic profile rather than a
single stability score. This is important because explanation drift is
multi-dimensional. A large local drift value has a different interpretation
depending on whether it coincides with covariate drift, target drift,
performance changes, or high within-window instability.

The same common evaluation slice is used when comparing model versions \(A\)
and \(B\) within a window pair. For a given model--explainer combination, both
model versions are explained on the same selected instances, making the
attribution distances instance-wise comparable. When the flagged set is defined
using model-specific prediction scores, however, the selected instances may
differ across model families. Cross-model comparisons should therefore be
interpreted with this in mind: they compare the stability behaviour of each
model in its own operationally relevant subset, rather than on one universal
flagged set shared by all models.

The logistic-regression coefficient reference is reported separately. For this
transparent model, the analysis focuses on within-window coefficient instability
and on the change in full-window coefficient vectors between model versions,
rather than on local post-hoc explanation drift or explainer stochasticity.








\section{Implementation}
\label{sec:implementation}

This section describes how the proposed methodology is instantiated in the
empirical study. The full codebase is available in the accompanying GitHub
repository.\footnote{\url{https://github.com/FreWlms/Temporal_XAI_Stability/tree/main}}
The implementation is organised around three datasets from the TabReD benchmark.
For each dataset, the same general rolling-retraining and explanation-stability
framework is applied, while dataset-specific choices such as the timestamp,
preprocessing recipe, window configuration, and model settings are documented
separately.


\subsection{Use of the TabReD Benchmark}
\label{subsec:impl_tabred}

The empirical study is based on datasets from the TabReD benchmark 
\cite{rubachev_tabred_2024}. TabReD is well suited to this thesis for two
reasons.

First, TabReD was designed to make tabular machine-learning evaluation more
representative of real-world deployment settings. In particular, the benchmark
emphasises temporal splits, realistic feature engineering, and industrial-style
tabular prediction tasks. This makes it a natural fit for studying explanation
stability under rolling retraining, where the temporal ordering of observations
is central to the research question.

Second, TabReD provides preprocessing and feature-engineering pipelines for its
datasets. This is useful because the focus of this thesis is not to design new
domain-specific features, but to evaluate how explanations behave when models
are retrained over time. By relying on the TabReD preprocessing recipes, we can
work with realistic fixed-width feature matrices while keeping the experimental
focus on temporal explanation stability.

We therefore use TabReD in two ways. The benchmark motivates the choice of
datasets, and its preprocessing scripts serve as the starting point for the
feature construction. We do not, however, adopt TabReD's static benchmark
protocol directly. The train/test construction is replaced by the rolling-window
setup described in Section~\ref{sec:rolling_setup}.


\subsection{Implementation 1: Acquire Valued Shoppers Challenge}
\label{subsec:impl_shoppers}

The first implemented dataset is the \emph{Acquire Valued Shoppers Challenge}
dataset. This is a binary classification task in which the goal is to predict
whether a customer who received a personalised offer will become a repeat buyer.
Each observation corresponds to a customer--offer pair, and the target indicates
whether the customer made a qualifying return purchase after receiving the
offer.

\subsubsection{Preprocessing}

The raw dataset consists of a customer--offer table, an offer catalogue, and a
large transaction log. We follow the feature-construction logic of TabReD's
\texttt{ecom-offers.py} preprocessing script to convert these relational data
into a fixed-width tabular representation, but adapt the temporal transaction
filters to exclude post-offer purchases from the predictor variables.

The resulting features summarise each customer's historical purchasing behaviour
before the offer date. Transaction histories are aggregated over several
look-back windows and over product scopes such as the offered company,
category, and brand. These behavioural aggregates are combined with offer-level
features and binary indicators describing whether the customer had previously
purchased from the relevant company, category, or brand.

The final processed dataset contains 119 predictors: 113 numeric features and
6 binary indicator features. The numeric features include transaction-spend
aggregates, offer value, offer-date variables, and look-back-window aggregates
over the relevant company, category, and brand scopes. The binary features
summarise whether the customer had previously purchased in the corresponding
company, category, and brand scopes. These processed artefacts are saved once
and used by all downstream notebooks.

A substantial share of customers has no historical transaction record in the
source log before the offer date. For these observations, behavioural aggregate
features are zero-valued and the corresponding ``never bought'' indicators are
active. This is a meaningful state rather than missing data, but it creates a
large point mass at zero for several aggregate features, which should be kept in
mind when interpreting Wasserstein-based covariate drift.

\subsubsection{Temporal Setup}

For this implementation, the rolling-window configuration is:
\[
L = 5, \qquad s = 2, \qquad h = 2,
\]
where \(L\) is the training-window length in weeks, \(s\) is the temporal gap
between the two model versions compared within a pair, and \(h\) is the
evaluation horizon. Thus, each model is trained on five
weeks of data, adjacent model versions are separated by two weeks, and both are
evaluated on the same two-week future slice.

Weekly grouping provides a compromise between temporal resolution and the
number of observations per time step. Because the Shoppers offer period covers
only about two months, this configuration yields two valid rolling
comparisons; the results for this dataset should therefore be interpreted as a
case study rather than as a long temporal time series.

\subsubsection{Models}

The Shoppers implementation uses three model families:
\begin{itemize}
    \item logistic regression, used as a transparent reference model;
    \item XGBoost, used as a strong tree-based tabular model;
    \item MLP-PLR, used as a neural tabular model with numerical feature
          embeddings.
\end{itemize}

Logistic regression coefficients are used as a model-level reference for
coefficient stability. XGBoost is explained using TreeSHAP. MLP-PLR is explained
using a gradient-based SHAP explainer. LIME explanations are computed on a
subsample of the selected flagged instances because of their higher
computational cost.

\subsubsection{Replica Training and Hyperparameter Tuning}

For each window pair, hyperparameters are tuned separately for model version
\(A\) and model version \(B\). Tuning is performed within the corresponding
training window using a 3-fold \texttt{TimeSeriesSplit} cross-validation, with
average precision (PR-AUC) as the optimisation criterion. Hyperparameter
search is performed with Optuna using a TPE sampler, with \(50\), \(15\), and
\(20\) trials per window for XGBoost, logistic regression, and MLP-PLR
respectively. The exact search-space bounds for each family are documented in
the accompanying repository. The selected hyperparameters are then kept fixed
across all \(R = 8\) replicas of that model version.

Replica variability is introduced through class-stratified bootstrap sampling
and model-specific random seeds. The same seeding convention is used across
model families, so that replica indices are aligned as much as possible across
the XGBoost, logistic-regression, and MLP-PLR implementations.

XGBoost is trained directly on the processed feature matrix, with a hard cap
of \(2000\) boosting rounds and early stopping after \(50\) rounds without
validation-PR-AUC improvement. Logistic regression is implemented as a
pipeline of a per-replica \texttt{StandardScaler} and an \(L_2\)-regularised
logistic regression (\texttt{max\_iter} \(= 500\); \texttt{solver} =
\texttt{newton-cholesky} where available, otherwise \texttt{lbfgs}). Each
replica therefore fits its own scaler on its bootstrap sample, and the saved
coefficient vector lives in that replica's standardised basis. MLP-PLR uses
the same per-replica standardisation for numeric features, while binary
indicator features are passed through unchanged. MLP-PLR replicas are trained
with AdamW, a batch size of \(1024\), a maximum of \(50\) epochs, and patience
\(10\) on validation PR-AUC for early stopping; tuning trials use a tighter
\(20\)-epoch cap.

For XGBoost and MLP-PLR, the final replica training uses the most recent
\(15\%\) of each training window as a chronological early-stopping validation
tail. Bootstrap samples are drawn from the remaining training portion.
Logistic regression does not use early stopping and is bootstrapped from the
full training window.
In addition to these bootstrap replicas, one deterministic logistic-regression
model is fitted on each complete training window. The coefficient vectors of
these full-window models are used for the visual comparison of how the
transparent model changes between versions \(A\) and \(B\).

\subsubsection{Explanation Generation}

SHAP explanations are generated for XGBoost and MLP-PLR, while logistic
regression is represented by its fitted coefficient vectors. For XGBoost, we
use TreeSHAP on the full flagged set and on the raw model margin, so the
resulting SHAP values decompose the log-odds score rather than the predicted
probability. For MLP-PLR, we use a gradient-based SHAP explainer applied to the
pre-sigmoid logit. Because this computation is substantially more expensive,
MLP-PLR SHAP is computed on the same rank-spread subsample of the flagged set
used for LIME. Numeric features are transformed with the scaler belonging to
the corresponding MLP-PLR replica before explanation, while binary features are
passed through unchanged. The background set for each MLP-PLR SHAP explainer is
sampled from the corresponding training window.

LIME explanations are computed for XGBoost and MLP-PLR. In the Shoppers
implementation, the LIME subsample is capped at 200 instances per window pair,
and each LIME explanation uses 1000 perturbed samples. The same LIME subsample
is used for model versions \(A\) and \(B\) within a pair. The LIME perturbation
basis is window-specific: explanations for window \(A\) replicas use the raw
training data from window \(A\), while explanations for window \(B\) replicas
use the raw training data from window \(B\). The model prediction functions
receive raw feature vectors; any scaling required by MLP-PLR is handled inside
the model wrapper. The six binary indicator features are declared as
categorical features in LIME so that they are sampled as discrete variables
rather than as continuous quantities.


\subsubsection{Explained Instances and Metrics}

For each window pair and model family (XGBoost and MLP-PLR), explanations are computed on the
flagged set \(\mathcal{F}_{A,B}\) using \(k = 0.10\), i.e. on
\(\lceil 0.10\,|\mathcal{E}_{A,B}|\rceil\) evaluation instances ranked by the
maximum of the two replica-averaged predicted positive-class probabilities.
This focuses the analysis on instances considered relevant by at least one of
the two model versions.

The drift-analysis notebook combines the saved predictions, post-hoc attribution
tensors, and logistic-regression coefficient vectors into the quantities
reported in the results chapter: covariate drift, target drift, predictive
performance, local explanation drift for SHAP and LIME, within-window baseline
instability, global SHAP drift, explainer-stochasticity diagnostics, and the
transparent logistic-regression coefficient reference.

For comparability in the coefficient-stability analysis, the saved coefficient
vectors used in the drift-analysis notebook are re-expressed in a common
pair-level reference basis. Numeric coefficients are expressed per reference
standard deviation, while binary coefficients remain on their raw \(0/1\)
scale. The raw per-replica coefficients are retained for traceability but are
not used directly for cross-window interpretation.
